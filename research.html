Passing the Buck: The Effects of a Targeted Tax Cut for Pass-Through Businesses

What are the effects of tax cuts for business owners on the labor market? Exploiting a change in Kansas state tax policy, this paper examines the labor market effects of eliminating taxes on pass-through income. I find that the tax cut reduced total employment by 2.3%, with smaller declines among pass-through businesses. Despite lower employment at pass-through firms, total payroll among these businesses did not fall, resulting in higher average earnings. In contrast, non-profit organizations experienced the largest employment declines. Further, the relative share of pass-through businesses increased following the reform. Together, these results suggest that tax reductions do not necessarily stimulate hiring and may instead induce organizational reallocation.

The Compensating Differentials of Remote Work

Remote work has become increasingly common following the COVID-19 pandemic, particularly in high-skill industries. Using longitudinal data from the Survey of Income and Program Participation, I estimate the compensating wage differential between remote and in-person work. Exploiting workers who switch between remote and in-person arrangements, I use a Bayesian causal forest approach to flexibly model wage dynamics while isolating within-worker treatment effects and allowing for heterogeneous responses across occupations and demographic groups.
